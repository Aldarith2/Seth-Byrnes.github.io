Ownership and Outcome

DinoHab is a mixed reality plus VR ecosystem management game. It was built around the challenge of making a living habitat feel reactive and worth caring for without being a chore, as well as educate young players about the paleolothic period. I was tasked with and owned the central ecological systems. I designed a 'Harmony' feature that required players to manage plant, fungi and waste while contributing to buddy pet and creature interaction through behavioural state tree design. I also supported tutorialization, minigame design,narrative wireframing, milestone planning,implementation review and QA testing across the entire production. My contributions shipped as part of the public 2024 Meta release and the game is available on the Quest2 and 3 headsets.

Responsibilities

  • Owned the ecology (plant, fungi, waste, and harmony) feature set from design through implementation, testing, and balancing through to release.
  • Authored a 27-page plant systems design document complete with implementation specs covering growth states, plant propagation, nutrient logic, ground cover and dormancy.
  • Co-developed the project balancing workbook, programming roughly half of the balancing across a shared 9-tab spreadsheet with approximately 355 formulas governing ecosystem, species, and progression.
  • Authored pet dinosaur state trees and wrote spec documents outlining functions surrounding trust, player acknowledgement, needs based satisfaction, growth stages, and reward rules for player-animal interaction.
  • Contributed to the design of the players core multitool and interaction mechanics.
  • Mapped tutorial flow, teaching player tools and feature introduction for both MR and VR play.
  • Supported milestone planning through extensive wireframing for both gameplay and narrative progression
  • Performed implementation review and iteration throughout the projects lifespan.

Examples

As a hybrid edutainment ecological and pet raising simulator,DinoHab had interesting project considerations.

Creating a visible ecosystem loop

The game needed a core ecological loop that was visible to the player. Plants would not grow on their own, they needed time and ideal conditions. Waste would not only be a cleanup chore - it was a resource and a fertilizer. I wanted DinoHab to make that loop legible. Connecting Fungi and Plants was where I spent a lot of that effort - they let me close the circle between what dinosaurs consume, what they produce, and how the habitat recovers from grazing and weather.

Plants were simple entities that appeared complex. When planted, they started life as a sapling and progressed through 4 stages of life. Over time they generated 'Nutrients' - a resource that the Dinosaurs require to be happy. The nutrient valuer stored in a plant dictates its size and stage of life, therefore allowing it to move between states while dinosaurs graze. Without enough plants in the ecosystem, all would be grazed to nothing by hungry Dinos - giving the player incentive to earn DinoPoints to plant more. By making life stages, interactions between fungi and waste compost transfer visible, the harmony system could be understood as a closed loop. I could give the player a clear read on why the ecosystem was healthy or unhealthy. The best part was that this solved both a design problem and a UX problem at the same time: it made plant and waste resources strategically useful instead of just a chore, and it gave the player a fast read on whether they needed more flora, more fungi, or a different layout.

Making placement more than decorative

Eating, making waste and recycling can only be so exciting. Planting the same thing over and over was not a sustainable core loop - and we needed to make the player to do more than just fill out the world with ferns. To give players meaningful habitat decisions, they needed to see that planting worked in some locations and in others failed. I designed a system where each plant had a placement radii, cover values, growth stages, and propagation rules. This made plants feel alive rather than just background dressing. This also gave me something to tie in to another feature...

I designed plants to have needs, much like dinosaurs. Each category of plant had likes and dislikes, and preferred to be planted in proximity to environmental features that benefited it. This meant we could now teach players about not only the relationship between animals and their sources of food, but also of plants and their relationship with the environment itself.

What I found to be effective was to make the systems logic visible enough that players could build intuition around what was happening. Scanning the plants and animals would give a synopsis of their needs, coupled with visible states and placement feedback, and an obvious relationships between growth and consumption, the environment suddenly became readable and manageable. It also gave me as a designer better tuning hooks, because the same plant feature set could support progression, interaction, and visuals all at the same time.

Trying to teach through observation and state trees

One of the risks in a habitat game like this is leaning too hard on UI and numerical feedback, which can kill the fantasy of caring for a living creature. I wanted the dinosaurs and plants both to feel alive, but I did not want them to feel like vending machines with all the numbers attached. Explicit state logic for hunger, water, rest, social interaction, satisfaction, and growth had to be plotted so the creatures could perform their life cycle in a way that the player would observe naturally.

The buddy creature was especially useful because it gave the player somebody to observe during onboarding. Instead of relying on constant prompts, I could use the buddy to guide the player through it's needs by telegraphing with playful hops to the thing which it wanted most. This way the player could discover what the dinosaurs wanted organically, reinforcing whatever the tutorial had to say about the ecosystem through observable state change.

Shaking things up for the dinos

Once the habitat loop was working, the game could became very passive. Players only had to hit the minimum requirements once and then they entered a positive feedback loop that never ended. We needed some way to shake the system up occasionally and so I proposed we create some kind of environmental calamity. I called these 'Disruptors' internally, and used them to break the positive feedback loop. Inclement weather could kill plants and animals, and predator events forced the player to try to build a habitat that could absorb stress.

The ground cover threshold that Dinosaurs required for sleep also became a mechanic that hid them from predators, incentivizing players to ensure that every dinosaur had a safe place to hide in every area should a hungry tyrannosaur stumble into the level. I liked this approach because it continued to be education - predation exists in nature, and shelter, water access, and habitat layout suddenly matterred under pressure just as in the real world. That closed one of the last loops required to make the ecosystem feel less like a static puzzle and more like something alive and changing where the player had a special role as a steward.
