Ownership and Outcome

The Deep: Beyond the Reef is a VR underwater adventure game. I took over a proof of concept with unrelated mechanics in early 2025 and was tasked with turning it into a complete pitch. I designed the new pitch to be built around exploration, hazard avoidance, puzzles, and environmental cleanup - all these things were important facets of Tim Thompsons original work. In pre-production, I defined the project’s core loop, the players tools, movement and progression structure. With those in development, I tackled level design - a series of hubs unlocked by solving puzzles - hazard design, and narrative wireframing. The team and I carried those designs forward through several iterations to testing final configuration, and level art support as the game evolved. The result is a publicly released as a Meta store title and my work is now a part of a major television IP that I am proud of.

Responsibilities

  • Defined and owned the core gameplay loop around exploration and 3D hazard avoidance, cleaning, puzzle challenges and gated upgrades in pre-production.
  • Produced 180+ pages of critical design material across more than 28 specification documents.
  • Designed more than 6 connected gameplay spaces, including a 3 hub zones with multiple sublevels. Grayboxed 4 levels and worked with the art and dev teams to finalize and ship them.
  • Wrote the tutorial onboarding path and designed player tools, HUD prototypes, puzzles, movement rules and environmental cleanup interactions.
  • Authored multiple feature briefs and implementation-facing documentation, specifying 80+ named gameplay variables and requesting design tuning parameters for locomotion, laser interaction, artifacts, contamination, puzzles, enemy behaviours, rewards, and feedback systems.
  • Structured and oversaw Quest 3 user testing and captured notes, proposing actionable changes for clarity and readability.
  • Supported producers throughout production planning, helping track and delegate tasks related to in-engine tuning, level design, implementation review, and project scope revision through to release.

Examples

Here are a few examples of my work on The Deep. We had four connected design problems: giving the player a meaningful tool for interacting, turning freeform swimming into exciting platforming style evasion, building puzzles that made sense within the games logic while staying true to the IP, and structuring progression to make sure discovery always led somewhere new and meaningful.

Linking cleaning mechanics and progression

One of the first problems I had to solve on The Deep was that the player would only have one main tool due to project limitations. It needed to feel cool to a young audience, but remain nonviolent and tactile due to needs of the IP. Enter the Sonic Laser - narratively a fundamentally acoustic tool that acts and feels much like a lightsaber under the right circumstances. Cleaning contamination was the main focus of the game, and had to be linked to upgrades, collectibles, gates, and puzzles which all behaved like separate systems. I worked to define the laser as a common verb across the entire game.

It cleaned contamination and revealed functional objects beneath, cleared pathways, grabbed objects, collected pickups, triggered certain puzzle states. It was also colour coded - its upgrade level determined which contamination colors the player could interact with, and this allowed us to gate routes whose doors the player could open once they had cleaned enough of the surrounding area to reveal the puzzle solutions.

With cleaning tied directly into progression, contamination was not just scenery; it hid plot critical artifacts, gated routes that controlled access to later areas, and could slow feed the player rewards such as health. With laser level and contamination color linked, exploration and upgrade discovery immediately changed what the player could interact with in the gameworld. These associations gave the game a consistent tactile language: when players saw an interactable color-coded puzzle covered in contamination, they knew the same underlying systems linked the elements together and this gave them implied goals.

Creating movement problems for the player to solve

The player could swim in any direction - this meant I could not rely on flat threat design. Hazards had to present a danger in three dimensions as well as needing to be understandable while moving at speed in VR. First, I decided to treat enemies as movement puzzles. Urchins, Jellyfish and Electric Eels all behaved as different forms of mobile area denial that forced the player to consider their spacial awareness and time their movement. To make this more interesting, I designed a speed boost mechanic so we could link movement and metrics together when designing levels.

The players quickly became adept at circumnaviating these semi-anchored enemeies. To keep players on their toes, I designed a Sharks with an awareness state tree. They could respond to sight and sound - if the player were to clean contamination, the shark would growl and move to investigate. If they got the player in their line of site, they would increase speed and attack. The sharks were first used in open water where their tracking and line of sight felt overpowered, so they were moved underground into a cave system to give the player more opportunities to break up line of sight. Late in the game we had to come up with ways to combine enemies in sequence to create interest. Urchins became far more useful when paired with moving threats, because they created moments where the player had to use their limited dodge charges to avoid obstacles while being pursued.

I wanted all these encounters to feel reactive without being punitive. In early designs the laser could slow hostile creatures or in some cases passive wildlife could be stunned. In the end, due to constraints we simplified this system so that contaminated wildlife would shift into a safer, less threatening state once cleaned. This gave the player agency over pressure and allowed them to reduce the threat level of a challenging slalom. Instead of the enemy hazards blocking progress, they became part of the same loop as cleaning and traversal.

Puzzles for inquisitive minds

The puzzle designs on The Deep: Beyond The Reef had to do more than simply block the player. I wanted puzzles reinforce the exploration and cleaning toolset the player was used to, and they needed to support the IP. Spelunking and treasure hunting are hallmarks of the original work BTR is based on. I used static reconstruction, keyed doors and plinths, and color-coded interactions to keep the solutions visually grounded for young players. Solving a puzzle always required using the laser to uncover a hidden element which then needed to be picked up and carried while moving through space, reading environmental queues that helped understandobject placement.

I was especially conscious of readability. The puzzles needed to feel approachable for younger players without being insulting. With the help of the art team, we created a visual hierarchy with components that were obviously related to one-another. When they were placed, satisfying audiovisual confirmation rewarded the player.

The visual layouts always suggested the answer before the game ever had to explain it. A clam without a pearl. A disc missing a component. A lit structure next to an unlit one. The goal was for players to create associations on their own, and feel that they had figured something out for themselves even when the artwork quietly did a lot of work to keep the solution readable.

Structuring narrative and progression across hubs

Once the core mechanics were working together, the last challenge was sequencing everything so the game kept pulling the player forward. I arranged the main narrative and gameplay beats on wireframe to map out the experience. I needed to spread out the progression across the major hubs so discovery felt steady without being too stale or too easy. The Reef served as reinforcement for the tutorial and as a central hub - teaching the core mechanics through problem solving while leaving enough openness for curiosity. Players could always see the doors before they found the keys, and color coding let them know when progress was made and when to return to key open a new door.

The Caves then reused the fundamental mechanics but in a different environment,introducing new contamination types, twists in the form of new enemy behaviors, and a new puzzle in every zone.

What mattered to me was that progression always felt like the world was opening up for a reason. Every artifact revealed, every new type of contamination the player blasted off a door needed to feel earned. Every puzzle solution had to point the player at the next worthwhile objective without holding their hand. When the level designs clicked, players could experience a sense of challenge while still understanding why they were there, where they were going what they were supposed to be doing.
