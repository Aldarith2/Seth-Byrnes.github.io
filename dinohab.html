Ownership and Outcome

DinoHab was a mixed reality and VR ecosystem management project built around the challenge of making a living habitat feel legible, reactive, and worth caring for without reducing it to disconnected meters. I owned the plant, fungi, waste, and harmony feature set, contributed creature interaction and buddy behaviour rules, and supported onboarding, wireframing, milestone planning, implementation review, testing, and configuration across production. The work focused on turning broad ecosystem concepts into readable player-facing systems and implementation-ready documentation that could survive iteration. That contribution shipped as part of the public 2024 Meta release.

Responsibilities

  • Owned the plant, fungi, waste, and harmony feature set from design through implementation oversight, testing, and release configuration.
  • Authored a 27-page plant systems design document covering growth states, propagation, nutrient logic, cover, dormancy, and implementation requirements.
  • Co-developed the project balancing workbook, programming roughly half of the logic across a shared 9-tab sheet with approximately 355 formulas for ecosystem, species, and progression tuning.
  • Authored dinosaur trust, acknowledgement, satisfaction, levelling, and reward rules for player-animal interaction.
  • Mapped tutorial flow, player tools, onboarding beats, and feature introduction for both MR and VR play.
  • Supported milestone planning, wireframes, implementation review, and iteration from playtest and QA feedback.

Examples

These samples show the work from two angles. The cards below explain the design problems I was trying to solve and why I approached them the way I did. The PDF examples that follow are the implementation-facing documents behind that work.

Turned harmony, fungi, and waste into one visible ecosystem loop

A lot of ecology games end up abstracting the interesting part away. Waste becomes a cleanup chore, harmony becomes a mystery number, and the player is left guessing which action actually matters. I wanted DinoHab to make that loop legible. Fungi and waste were where I spent a lot of that effort, because they let me close the circle between what dinosaurs consume, what they produce, and how the habitat recovers.

By making mycelium ranges, compost transfer, and harmony feedback visible, I could give the player a clear read on why the ecosystem was healthy or unstable. The nice part was that this solved both a design problem and a UX problem at once: it made dung strategically useful instead of just gross, and it gave the player a fast way to understand whether they needed more flora, more fungi, or a different layout.

Made plant placement readable instead of decorative

Plants had to do more than fill out the world. If players were going to make meaningful habitat decisions, they needed to understand why one planting choice worked and another failed. I used placement radii, occupied space, cover values, growth stages, and propagation rules to make plants behave like gameplay objects rather than background dressing. That gave players something they could reason about instead of just spam.

What I found effective here was making the logic visible enough that players could build intuition quickly. Scanning, placement feedback, and clear relationships between growth, cover, and surrounding objects helped the habitat read as a system. It also gave us better tuning hooks, because the same plant feature could support progression, creature needs, and visual recovery at the same time.

Built creature and buddy behaviours that taught the game naturally

One of the risks in a habitat game like this is leaning too hard on UI and losing the fantasy of caring for living creatures. I wanted the dinosaurs to feel readable, but I did not want them to feel like vending machines with moods attached. That pushed me toward explicit state logic for hunger, water, rest, social, satisfaction, trust, growth, and player interaction, so the creatures could react consistently without feeling scripted in a flat way.

The buddy was especially useful because it let me solve onboarding more organically. Instead of relying on constant prompts, I used follow behaviour, playful hops, objective-leading, and special MR rules so the companion could pull the player toward the next useful action and stay emotionally present across transitions. I liked this because it made guidance feel like part of the relationship rather than a layer floating on top of the game.

Used disruptors to test resilience instead of just waiting for harmony

Once the habitat started working, there was a danger that the game could become too passive. If players only had to hit the minimum requirements once, harmony risked turning into a solved machine. I used disruptors to break that pattern. Weather and predator-style events let the game ask whether the player had built a habitat that could absorb stress, not just one that looked balanced for a moment.

I liked this approach because it pushed preparation back onto the player in a readable way. Shelter, water access, plant mix, and habitat layout suddenly mattered under pressure, and progression stayed active instead of becoming a waiting game. That made the ecosystem feel less like a static puzzle and more like something the player had to maintain.
