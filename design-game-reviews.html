Favorite Games

This page separates the game writing from the shorter philosophy notes. None of these are presented as a sacred canon. They are here because they continue to influence how I think about readability, authored space, player trust, and systems that explain themselves.

They are also useful because each of them demonstrates something concrete: a strong title fantasy, a clear combat language, a disciplined interface, a memorable atmosphere, or a willingness to trust the player more than most modern productions do.

Thief: The Dark Project / Thief Gold

What it gets right: This remains one of the clearest cases of title, fantasy, and mechanics aligning perfectly. The game tells you what you are, what you want, and what will get you killed. Light, sound, and surface material are not side systems. They are the language of play.

Where it stumbles: Combat and climbing can be awkward, and some mission roughness is undeniable. None of that changes how elegantly it teaches stealth through authored spaces instead of tutorial noise.

Thief II: The Metal Age

What it gets right: Thief II keeps the stealth core but sharpens readability. The city, the mechanist threat, and the security fiction all give the game a cleaner mechanical identity. It is more modern, more urban, and in many ways more immediately usable than the first game.

Where it stumbles: It loses some of the strange occult edge that gives the first game so much texture. The trade is worth it more often than not, but it is still a trade.

Thief: Deadly Shadows

What it gets right: Deadly Shadows is a smart consolidation of the series. It understands social stealth, city presence, and physical intimacy in a way the earlier games only hinted at. The Cradle alone is enough to explain why the game still matters.

Where it stumbles: Loading breaks space apart and some simplification was clearly driven by platform limits. Even so, it preserves the fantasy better than many sequels preserve anything.

Deus Ex

What it gets right: Deus Ex is still a benchmark for authored systemic space. It gives the player multiple routes through a problem, but those routes remain grounded in believable architecture, faction tension, and place. It feels less like a puzzle box and more like a compromised real world.

Where it stumbles: Some weapon feel, balance, and augmentation value are dated. Its density of possibility still compensates for almost all of it.

Morrowind

What it gets right: Morrowind trusts the player. It expects reading, attention, and a willingness to inhabit an alien place instead of chasing icons. That confidence is a large part of why it still feels authored rather than optimized.

Where it stumbles: Early combat feedback is weak, onboarding is harsh, and some opacity is friction instead of mystery. The world is specific enough to survive those costs.

UT2004

What it gets right: UT2004 is a celebration of readable speed. Movement, weapon roles, announcer callouts, and vehicle combat all remain legible at ridiculous pace. Onslaught in particular is one of the best examples of a large-scale arcade war mode still communicating clearly.

Where it stumbles: Not every mutator or map is equally disciplined, and the excess can work against the game. Its peaks are still absurdly high.

Half-Life

What it gets right: Half-Life remains one of the best lessons in environmental sequencing and player onboarding. Black Mesa teaches through space, motion, and escalating context rather than stopping to lecture the player.

Where it stumbles: Some later combat passages overstay their welcome and some bottlenecks show their age. As a communication piece, it is still elite.

Doom

What it gets right: Doom is an almost perfect argument for mechanical clarity. Enemy silhouettes, weapon roles, keys, doors, damage states, and combat rhythm all read instantly. It is fast, violent, and simple in the exact way it needs to be.

Where it stumbles: Its fiction is mostly delivery framing, and some episodes are much stronger than others. None of that matters once the fight language starts working.

Doom II

What it gets right: Doom II expands the monster vocabulary in a way that permanently changed encounter design. The super shotgun alone justifies its existence, and the larger roster gives mappers far more expressive combat composition.

Where it stumbles: The campaign is uneven and some levels are strange for the wrong reasons. It still became the dominant sandbox because the toolset it handed players was so strong.

Age of Empires

What it gets right: Age of Empires is elegant in the way it frames advancement. Gathering, aging up, and unit counters are understandable quickly, and the game sells historical sweep without burying the player in interface noise.

Where it stumbles: Later RTS games improved pathfinding, formations, and usability. The original still matters because the core progression fantasy is so clean.

Age of Empires II

What it gets right: Age of Empires II is a refinement masterpiece. Better readability, stronger interface language, cleaner villager management, and sharper faction identities make it one of the most durable RTS designs ever shipped.

Where it stumbles: Balance conversations never really end and some civs are more charming than elegant. It remains an astonishingly stable foundation for competitive and casual play alike.

Empire Earth

What it gets right: Empire Earth understands the pleasure of massive historical scale. The epoch ladder gives the player a silly, satisfying sweep from ancient warfare to absurd futurism, but it keeps the RTS language familiar enough to stay playable.

Where it stumbles: That scale can flatten identity and create muddy balance. It is still one of the clearest examples of excess being genuinely fun.

Rune by Human Head Studios

What it gets right: Rune makes melee feel brutish, physical, and readable. Weapon weight, severing, thrown axes, and even the indignity of being hit with a ripped-off limb all contribute to a clean, ugly combat language that fits its tone.

Where it stumbles: It is not graceful and it is not subtle. That is also part of why it works.

Diablo I

What it gets right: Diablo is almost impossibly legible. Three classes, one town, one cathedral, one downward spiral. It is compact enough that every descent feels meaningful and every drop matters.

Where it stumbles: Repetition is part of the structure, and some of the later balance is crude. The economy of the whole thing is still extraordinary.

Soul Reaver

What it gets right: Soul Reaver turns realm-shifting into architectural language. The atmosphere, performance, and theatrical writing do an enormous amount of work, but the real achievement is making the level design itself feel like a metaphysical argument.

Where it stumbles: Its ending is abrupt and its puzzle cadence can stall the momentum. The world remains one of the strongest gothic spaces games ever built.

Soul Reaver II

What it gets right: Soul Reaver II is more narrative, more controlled, and more interested in temporal mythmaking than the first game. It trades some of the raw exploratory power of Soul Reaver for precision in writing and character revelation.

Where it stumbles: It narrows the action and can feel constrained after the first game. As a piece of directed dramatic design, it is still excellent.

Defiance

What it gets right: Defiance is valuable because it finally lets Kain and Raziel share the same mechanical frame. It is more direct, more combat-driven, and more eager to synthesize the split halves of the larger series.

Where it stumbles: It loses some of the more meditative exploratory identity of the earlier games. Even so, it gives the saga a muscular final shape that I still admire.

Blood Omen 2

What it gets right: Blood Omen 2 has a specific, unpleasant confidence to it. Kain feels like a predator first and a hero never, which is exactly what he should feel like. The feeding, domination, and urban cruelty all serve that tone.

Where it stumbles: It is mechanically rough and narratively less elegant than the Amy Hennig-led entries. Its attitude still carries it a long way.

Omikron: The Nomad Soul

What it gets right: Omikron is gloriously overambitious. Body-hopping, first-person shooting, adventure exploration, martial arts, dystopian city fiction, and David Bowie should not cohere at all, and yet the game remains memorable precisely because it tries too much.

Where it stumbles: Controls, combat transitions, and general cohesion are messy. I still respect fever-project ambition when it aims at something specific.

Dark Forces II

What it gets right: Dark Forces II captures the transitional joy of first-person action becoming lightsaber fantasy. The Force powers, live-action melodrama, and branching moral pressure make it feel larger than its immediate gunplay.

Where it stumbles: It is awkward in the way many late-1990s hybrids are awkward. That awkwardness is attached to a very real sense of authorship.

Dwarf Fortress

What it gets right: Dwarf Fortress treats simulation as a story engine rather than a balance layer. The generated history, material interactions, injuries, moods, and catastrophes all contribute to a game that is constantly becoming anecdote.

Where it stumbles: Readability is often an acquired skill rather than a default quality. Once learned, it becomes one of the most authorially generous games ever made.
