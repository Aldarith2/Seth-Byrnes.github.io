Ownership and Outcome

Monster Simulator 3000 is a first-person arcade style rogue-lite VR kaiju smash-em-up game. It is built for exciting traversal, environmental destruction, wave combat, and run-based escalation. My work was all about making runs more fun and replayable and I took that on mid-production. I designed wave based encounters, enemy and boss behaviour, designed and tweaked perk-driven progression, scoring systems,revised physics and throw behaviour, owning documentation, implementation oversight, testing, and polish. The project shipped as a strong, fun arcade-style experience in late 2024 on the Oculus Quest 2 and 3, and is available on the Meta store.

Responsibilities

  • Authored 16+ design documents covering level workflow and design, encounter & enemy behaviour, world behavior and destruction, and gameplay tuning.
  • Produced 5+ level workflow and production documents, 7 combat and encounter documents, 2 world-simulation documents, and 2 tuning documents capturing all the important configuation points.
  • Created level briefs and production documentation to help onboard teammates.
  • Wrote more than 7 combat and encounter documents defining behavior for tanks, helicopters, blimps, turrets and boss variations for each,taxonomic weapon randomization, win conditions, player movement, and VR interaction rules.
  • Defined scoring rules, throw behaviour / physics, spawn wave logic, perk scaling, stats tracking, and player-facing UI feedback to support a quick moving arcade style loop.
  • Designed civilian and civilian vehicle simulation behaviours to make the city feel alive during the core combat loop.
  • Consolidated all late-stage configuration points across player, scoring, randomisation, and enemy systems, into a large implementation-facing Configuration Points document, the biggest visible authored document in the design book (It had a 27-minute read estimate!).

Each run needed a little something/h3>

The first problem Monster Simulator had when I arrived in 2024 was that each run felt similar to the last. Smashing buildings and eating people as fast as your arms can move feels good almost instantly, but that also means the kaiju fantasy falls flat by the third level if nothing new happens. I wanted the action to feel immediate and arcadey, but I also wanted every run to have it's own 'excitement parabola'. The player’s fists have to keep dribbling to move, except instead of controlling a ball they are smashing, throwing, and flinging their body across the city while looking for an opening to score. The place my head kept returning to was basketball: the player is effectively a humanoid character with elongated super arms trying to get a ball (Power Cell) to the Basket (Reactor) and dunk it to trigger a meltdown. That meant the run needed a rhythm.

One of the first things I changed was having Perks spawn during a run instead of being chosen in between rounds. This sped up gameplay significantly and eliminated some of the need for a currency. The perks were stratified into 'slots' (Breath, Arms and Passive) and upgraded specific attacks but disappeared on death. That forced the player to change tactics with the emergent gameplay and made a run feel like every run was an evolving situation. More importantly, it created a flexible play state we could change over time. Next came the need to make enemy pressure, boss behaviour and difficulty dynamic and give the player the sense that the rest of the court was reacting to their push on the net.

The soup needed spice

Once the randomized player progression layer was in place, the next issue was combat. In first-person VR, players usually test every physical interaction they can think of. If the player discovers one thing can be grabbed, the player will try to grab everything. This risks the whole game starts to feel inconsistent if an interaction does not get reused. Since I knew the game was going to sustain that basketball-like drive, then the court also needed reactive obstacles. Pickups were sorted but buildings, enemies, bosses and the main beats had to support the rhythm of approach, impact, recovery, and escape.

By treating enemies taxonomically we would be able to reuse the same four enemies but have multiple variations. Tanks, helicopters, blimps, turrets would all come in different flavours. Green was the standard colour, but Ice, Fire, Lighting and Elite variants of each would also receive a role. Enemies with shields would provide cover for their underlings, and the lighting enemies could stun and disable the hands you needed to throw an errant helicopter through their shield to destroy them. The enemies were now dynamic players trying to block your way to the basket - different shapes but all formidible enough that the player had to think about which perks to keep, and which to let go of.

Last but not least

After that, bosses became the place where I could ask the player to demonstrate what they had learned. My main concern was readability. At kaiju scale in VR, a fight can look dramatic and still feel unfair if the player cannot tell what is about to happen, when the danger begins, or when they are supposed to counter. I treated each boss as a more deliberate version of the same rules the player had already been learning during the run: movement pattern, telegraph, punishable opening, and recovery state. In the basketball comparison, these are the set-piece possessions where the defense is organized and the player has to earn the lane instead of simply sprinting through open space.

That is why these fights lean so heavily on visible shield drops, movement routes, reticle locks, charging sounds, defensive phases, and vulnerability windows. The Boss Blimp teaches timing through repeated bombing paths and bomb return play. The Boss Helicopter teaches anticipation through arc movement and stop phases. The Boss Tank teaches commitment through clear charge-up cues and throw timing. What I like about this set is that each boss solves the same problem from a different angle, which makes them feel like the natural next step in the run instead of a disconnected mode switch. They are essentially set plays that force the player to read pressure, create space, and commit to the right moment to finish.

Finally tuned escalation so the whole run stayed readable under pressure

Once progression, enemy rules, and bosses were all doing their jobs, the final task was keeping the full run from collapsing into noise. I generally try to avoid difficulty curves that rely only on inflating health or damage, especially in a game already full of destruction and visual clutter. Here, I wanted escalation to feel like the situation was changing around the player in response to performance, position, and momentum. The basketball comparison helped here too: the best runs have pace changes, pressure, recoveries, and a strong final drive, not just a louder version of the opening minute.

That is where spawn tables, state changes, objective triggers, boss damage phases, perk tuning, and wave conditions came together. A strong player could be met with tighter compositions or narrower openings. A struggling player could be given more room to recover. That balancing pass is what linked the whole sequence together for me: progression shaped the player, enemy rules shaped decision-making, bosses tested mastery, and escalation kept the run tense without tipping it into unreadable chaos. By the end, the player should feel less like they are wandering through random destruction and more like they are driving the lane through a crowded court, finding the Power Cell, and committing to the dunk before the defense closes in.
