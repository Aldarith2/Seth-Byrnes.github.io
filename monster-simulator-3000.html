Ownership and Outcome

Monster Simulator 3000 is a first-person VR kaiju destruction game built around traversal, urban destruction, enemy pressure, and run-based escalation. My work focused on making that chaos more readable, systemic, and replayable across production. I shaped encounter structure, enemy behaviour, perk-driven progression, objective logic, scoring, throw behaviour, spawn rules, boss patterns, and late-stage balancing, while owning enemy-design features from documentation through implementation oversight, testing, configuration, and polish. The project shipped as a stronger arcade-style destruction experience, supported by production-facing design documentation and tuning work that helped make the combat loop more legible, learnable, and sustainable across repeated runs.

Examples

Monster Simulator 3000 was named MonsterBash earlier in production. The verified authored material for this project covers level workflow, encounters and enemies, world simulation, and tracking and tuning, with roughly 77 minutes of visible authored read time across the recovered document set. The samples shown here focus on enemy and boss work, but they sit inside a broader body of implementation-facing design documentation that I designed, owned, oversaw in implementation, tested, and configured before release. I have used them here the same way I would talk through work in an interview: what problem I was trying to solve, what I changed, and why I think the result worked.

Started by giving each run a progression spine

The first problem I wanted to solve was sameness. Monster Simulator 3000 feels good very quickly, but that also means the fantasy can flatten out just as quickly if every run asks the player to do the same thing with the same tools. I wanted the action to stay immediate and arcadey, but I also wanted each session to develop its own shape. The comparison I kept returning to was basketball: the player is effectively an oversized power forward trying to get a Power Cell to the Reactor and dunk it to trigger a meltdown. That meant the run needed a rhythm, not just destruction. The player’s fists keep a kind of dribble going, except instead of controlling a ball they are smashing, throwing, and flinging their body across the city while looking for an opening to score.

Perks spawned during a run, upgraded specific attack slots, altered moves like Breath and Slam, and disappeared on death. That gave the player a reason to adapt and made a run feel like it was evolving instead of simply accumulating score. More importantly, it created a backbone for the rest of the game. Once player state could change over time, enemy pressure, boss behaviour, and difficulty tuning all had something clearer to react to, much like the rest of the court reacting once a player finds momentum and starts pushing toward the net.

Then defined enemy rules the player could actually learn

Once the progression layer was in place, the next issue was combat language. In first-person VR, players will test every physical interaction they can think of. If one unit can be grabbed, another rebounds unpredictably, and a third breaks for reasons the player cannot read, the whole game starts to feel inconsistent. If the run was going to sustain that basketball-like forward drive, then the court also needed readable obstacles. Buildings, enemy units, and pickups all had to support the same rhythm of approach, impact, recovery, and advance toward the Reactor.

I solved that by treating enemies taxonomically instead of as isolated one-offs. Tanks, helicopters, blimps, turrets, and elite variants each needed their own role, but they also needed to answer the same core questions consistently: what happens if I slap this, grab it, crush it, throw it, or shake it apart? Establishing those shared rules made the combat more legible, gave engineering and content design a stronger framework to build on, and let progression matter because the player could now form reliable instincts as the run escalated. In effect, the enemies became defenders on the way to the basket: different shapes of resistance, but all readable enough that the player could improvise a route through them.

Used bosses to turn that combat language into set-piece tests

After that, bosses became the place where I could ask the player to demonstrate what they had learned. My main concern was readability. At kaiju scale in VR, a fight can look dramatic and still feel unfair if the player cannot tell what is about to happen, when the danger begins, or when they are supposed to counter. I treated each boss as a more deliberate version of the same rules the player had already been learning during the run: movement pattern, telegraph, punishable opening, and recovery state. In the basketball comparison, these are the set-piece possessions where the defense is organized and the player has to earn the lane instead of simply sprinting through open space.

That is why these fights lean so heavily on visible shield drops, movement routes, reticle locks, charging sounds, defensive phases, and vulnerability windows. The Boss Blimp teaches timing through repeated bombing paths and bomb return play. The Boss Helicopter teaches anticipation through arc movement and stop phases. The Boss Tank teaches commitment through clear charge-up cues and throw timing. What I like about this set is that each boss solves the same problem from a different angle, which makes them feel like the natural next step in the run instead of a disconnected mode switch. They are essentially set plays that force the player to read pressure, create space, and commit to the right moment to finish.

Finally tuned escalation so the whole run stayed readable under pressure

Once progression, enemy rules, and bosses were all doing their jobs, the final task was keeping the full run from collapsing into noise. I generally try to avoid difficulty curves that rely only on inflating health or damage, especially in a game already full of destruction and visual clutter. Here, I wanted escalation to feel like the situation was changing around the player in response to performance, position, and momentum. The basketball comparison helped here too: the best runs have pace changes, pressure, recoveries, and a strong final drive, not just a louder version of the opening minute.

That is where spawn tables, state changes, objective triggers, boss damage phases, perk tuning, and wave conditions came together. A strong player could be met with tighter compositions or narrower openings. A struggling player could be given more room to recover. That balancing pass is what linked the whole sequence together for me: progression shaped the player, enemy rules shaped decision-making, bosses tested mastery, and escalation kept the run tense without tipping it into unreadable chaos. By the end, the player should feel less like they are wandering through random destruction and more like they are driving the lane through a crowded court, finding the Power Cell, and committing to the dunk before the defense closes in.
