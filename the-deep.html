Ownership and Outcome

The Deep: Beyond the Reef was a VR underwater adventure built around exploration, environmental cleanup, discovery, and environmental transformation. In pre-production, I helped define the project’s core loop, player tools, onboarding, progression structure, hazard design, and UI direction, then carried that work forward through implementation review, testing, configuration, and level design support as the game evolved. The result was a publicly released Meta store title with a clearer interaction language, structured progression, and a documented content path that supported the shipped experience.

Responsibilities

  • Defined and iterated the core gameplay loop around exploration, cleaning, challenge unlocking, upgrades, and forward progression.
  • Produced 180 pages of de-duplicated design material across 28 direct work-product documents covering progression, player tools, hazards, rewards, UI, and testing.
  • Designed and documented 6 connected gameplay spaces, including a 6-zone Reef hub, Yellow Cave, Red Cave, the Trench, Blue Cave, and the Monumental finale.
  • Built a 32-beat Reef onboarding path and designed player tools, HUD concepts, challenge-area structure, traversal rules, scanning behaviour, and environmental cleanup interactions in pre-production.
  • Authored 18 supporting feature briefs and implementation-facing documentation, including 80+ named gameplay variables and tuning parameters for locomotion, laser interaction, artifacts, contamination, puzzles, enemy behaviours, rewards, and feedback systems.
  • Structured Quest 3 user testing and translated findings into actionable changes for wayfinding, controls, feedback, puzzle clarity, and hazard readability.
  • Supported production planning, in-engine tuning, level design, implementation review, and live revision through internal testing and QA.

Examples

These examples focus on four connected design problems: giving the player one tactile language for interaction, turning freeform swimming into readable danger, building puzzles around the same core verbs, and structuring progression so discovery always led somewhere meaningful.

Creating a tactile language for cleaning, contamination, and progression

One of the first problems I had to solve on The Deep was that the player’s main tool could not feel detached from the rest of the game. If the Sonic Laser was only a cleaner, while upgrades, collectibles, gates, and puzzles all behaved like separate systems, the experience would fragment very quickly. I worked to make the laser the common verb across the game. It cleaned contamination, grabbed objects, collected pickups, triggered certain puzzle states, and its upgrade level determined which contamination colors and routes the player could meaningfully interact with.

That let me tie cleaning directly into progression. Contamination was not just scenery to remove; it hid artifacts, blocked routes, controlled access to later areas, and fed rewards such as energy crystals. Because laser level and contamination color were linked, every upgrade immediately changed what the player could do in the world. That gave the game a consistent tactile language: when players saw contamination, a collectible, or a blocked path, they were still reading the same underlying system rather than learning an unrelated one from scratch.

Creating movement problems and linking them to creature behaviour

Because the player could swim in any direction, I could not rely on flat, floor-based threat design. Hazards had to work in three dimensions and had to be understandable at speed in VR. I treated enemies and obstacles as movement problems first. Sharks were best in open water where their tracking and dives felt deliberate. Jellyfish and electric eels worked as mobile area denial that forced the player to read spacing and timing. Urchins became far more useful when paired with moving threats, because that created moments where the player had to decide whether to bait, dodge, clean, or reposition.

I also wanted those encounters to feel interactive rather than purely punitive. The laser could slow hostile creatures in some cases, passive wildlife could be stunned, and contaminated wildlife could shift into a safer state once cleaned. That mattered because it gave the player agency over pressure. Instead of hazards only stopping progress, they became part of the same loop as cleaning and traversal: the player learned the space, managed threat, cleaned strategically, and opened a safer route forward.

Puzzles that tied the core systems together

The puzzle work on The Deep had to do more than simply block doors. It needed to reinforce the same toolset the player was already learning through exploration and cleaning. I used reconstruct puzzles, beam puzzles, hidden pieces, keyed doors, and color-coded interactions so that solving a puzzle still meant using the laser, moving carefully through space, reading environmental clues, and understanding what the last upgrade had actually unlocked.

I was especially conscious of readability. These puzzles needed to feel approachable for younger players without becoming insultingly simple. That meant strong visual hierarchy, clear scan feedback, obvious snap points, satisfying audiovisual confirmation when a piece was placed correctly, and layouts that suggested the answer before the game ever had to explain it. The goal was for players to feel that they had figured something out for themselves, even when the system was quietly doing a lot of work to keep the solution legible.

Structuring discovery across hubs, upgrades, and narrative progression

Once the tools, hazards, and puzzles were working, the last challenge was sequencing them so the game kept pulling the player forward. I designed the main wireframe for the experience and laid out the progression across the major hubs so discovery felt steady rather than accidental. The Reef served as the onboarding hub, teaching the core verbs while leaving enough openness for curiosity. Later spaces such as the Trench and Monumental Zone then reused those verbs at a larger scale, with new contamination types, deeper puzzle chains, and stronger gating based on prior upgrades.

What mattered to me here was that progression should feel like the world was opening up for a reason. Artifacts, color-locked contamination, gated doors, creature encounters, and puzzle rooms all had to point at the next worthwhile objective without making the player feel dragged through a tunnel. When that structure was working, players could experience a sense of discovery while still understanding why they were there, what they had just earned, and what had become possible because of it.
