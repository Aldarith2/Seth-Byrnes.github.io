Ownership and Outcome

B-17: Flying Fortress The Bloody 100th was a WWII bomber simulation built around historical authenticity, mission context, crew-space readability, and grounded environmental reconstruction. My role sat between design, research, narrative support, and production coordination, with a strong focus on turning archival, museum, and primary-source material into buildable content for gameplay and art. In practice, that meant helping reconstruct Thorpe Abbotts as a usable production space rather than a loose historical impression, supporting mission and environment direction with structured reference, and giving the wider team clearer material to graybox, stage, and iterate from. The result was a stronger historical foundation for airbase layout, interiors, props, signage, and supporting mission-facing detail across the project.

Responsibilities

  • Built and organized a structured historical reference pipeline of 380+ production-useful items covering Thorpe Abbotts, USAAF operations, crew life, and broader bomber-support context.
  • Collaborated with museum contacts and primary-source archival material to convert research into usable design, environment, and mission-support reference.
  • Designed and documented mechanics, mission structures, and level concepts grounded in bomber operations, crew procedures, and period context.
  • Produced full airbase layout mockups and reference materials, including 30 targeted airfield-layout and structure references to support scale, placement, landmark relationships, and graybox decision-making.
  • Assembled 19 planning, document, and UI references, plus 18 aircraft, crew, and combat-context references, to support mission framing, environmental storytelling, and interface-adjacent authenticity.
  • Curated 15 uniform and personal-equipment references and 11 art-direction, color, and signage references to reduce ambiguity for props, materials, and other period-facing visual decisions.

Examples

This work started as research, but the useful part was turning that research into buildable space. I used archival photos, site plans, manuals, museum reference, and location material to help reconstruct Thorpe Abbotts, block key interiors, and give environment work a stronger historical foundation from graybox through final art.

Reconnaissance

The first task was finding the real Thorpe Abbotts in a way production could use. I worked with museum contacts and archival sources to locate genuine period aerial photographs of the airfield, then dug through period documents to work out the likely placement and purpose of buildings, roads, and support structures. The problem was not just authenticity. It was giving the team a real map of the place rather than a loose impression of it.

Once those references were cross-checked, they could be handed to artists as something actionable. That was the turning point. The airfield stopped being a vague historical subject and became a layout that could actually be plotted in engine and built from with confidence.

Grayboxing the base

Once the footprint was in place, period references started providing usable metrics for blockouts. We were able to reconstruct classrooms, briefing spaces, offices, and communal rooms, which meant we now had both interiors and exteriors to work with. At that stage, though, they were still hollow shells. The design problem became how to move from correct proportions to believable lived spaces.

This was where grayboxing proved its value. A room either read immediately or it did not. If the reference supported a clear blockout, then it was doing real production work. That process gave us spaces that could be tested, reviewed, and iterated on before final art, instead of relying on guesswork later.

Esoteric Material Culture

After that I went looking for the deep-cut details that made the base feel inhabited in 1944 rather than merely accurate from a distance. Personal accounts and photographs turned up the sort of references that are easy to miss but invaluable once found: improvised washroom details like reused coffee canisters, cigarette tins outside buildings, giant bomber scheduling chalkboards full of assignment information, and the plain Air Ministry furniture that gave offices and briefing rooms their period character.

I liked this stage because the findings were useful in more than one way. They helped environment art with the minutiae of daily life on the base, but they also informed mission design and interface thinking. The scheduling board, in particular, was not just dressing. It was a window into how bomber operations were organized and communicated.

Expanding the visual story on the base

Once the footprint, interiors, and material culture were established, the next step was broadening the visual story of the airfield itself. We wanted more than correct rooms. We wanted the player to feel transported into Thorpe Abbotts and its surrounding landscape. That meant pushing roughouts of interiors and exteriors further and using geographic surveys to reconstruct the countryside, roads, and wider spatial context of the time.

That wider context mattered because it tied the intimate spaces of the base to the operational life around them. The result was not just an accurate cluster of buildings, but a place with a horizon, a runway, a countryside, and a sense of routine that framed the missions themselves.

Research made into reality

One of the clearest examples of the whole process paying off was a mobile air traffic control hut that kept appearing in the background of reference photos. For a time we could see it often enough to know it mattered, but not clearly enough to understand it. Finding enough usable images to create a turnaround for the artists was a real research task in itself. Once we pinned down its role in communicating with aircraft as they taxied and landed, the model could finally move ahead with confidence.

I remember that moment vividly because it felt like research becoming tangible. There was a real buzz in the office when the model was finalized. It was a small structure, but it represented the best part of the project: obscure reference turning into a finished, placed object that made the world more believable.
