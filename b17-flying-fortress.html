Ownership and Outcome

B-17: Flying Fortress The Bloody 100th is a WWII bomber simulation built around historical authenticity, grounded environmental reconstruction, verisimilitude and a sense of intrepid adventure. My role was somewhere between design, research, narrative support, and production coordination. A lot of my time was spent on turning archival, museum, and primary-source material into buildable game design specs for development and art to execute. The main goal was to reconstruct Thorpe Abbotts airbase as it stood circa 1944 as an inhabitable space that felt alive rather than a loose backdrop set. We wanted the base to fully transport the player to the time and place at which the game was set. Using structured reference and giving the wider team a clear sense of the period, I sough to help graybox, stage, and iterate on our presentation of the American airbase in Great Britain during the second world war. The result was a strong and completely historical foundation for airbase layout with interiors, props, signage, and supporting mission-facing detail embedded in reconstructed material culture all over the base. I was very proud to bring this project from earliest conception to it's internal alpha state.

Responsibilities

  • I built and organized a structured historical reference pipeline of 380+ production-useful items covering Thorpe Abbotts, USAAF operations, crew life, and broader airbase logistical contexts.
  • Collaborated with several museum contacts to discover primary-source archival material to reasearch and convert into usable game design, environment, and mission-support briefs.
  • Designed and documented system mechanics, mission structures, and level concepts using first person accounts of bomber operations, crew procedures, and period photographic context.
  • Researched and documented material used as the basis for crew-management, mission, and progression systems intended to reflect bomber crew strain during a realistic 25 mission campaign.
  • Produced airbase layout reference materials and basic mockups, including more than 30 specific airfield-layout and structure references to support placement and geographic relationships of landmarks, and inform graybox decision-making.
  • Proposed mission-designer features that would allow players to build their own scenarios.
  • Performed in-engine tuning work on flight controls and level-design material in Unreal and Outerra.
  • All in all, I assembled around 19 planning, document and UI references, plus 18 aircraft, crew, and combat material culture references, to strengthen the historical authenticity of the projects environmental storytelling and visual presentation.
  • I curated 15 uniform and personal-equipment references and 11 art-direction, color (most images are black and white), and signage references to reduce ambiguity for props, materials, and other period material culture informed visual decisions.

Examples

This project started with a lot of front-heavy game design research, but the main task shortly became converting that research into a usable reference pipeline focused on buildable grayboxed space. I used archival photos, original site plans, manuals and other written references as well as museum archives to help reconstruct Thorpe Abbotts, inform blockouts on key interiors, and give environmentartists a stronger historical foundation to work with .

Macroreconnaissance

The first task was finding references for the real Thorpe Abbotts and framing them in a way production could use. I worked with museum contacts to gain access to archival sources. I was able to locate various genuine period aerial photographs of the airfield (which was a challenge since many extant images were censored in order to protect base infrastructure from the Germans), then dug through period documents to piece together likely placement and purpose of buildings, roads, and important support infrastructure. Giving the team a real map of the place rather was a difficult, if not impossible proposition. The only way to do it would be to cross reference all possible sources and create a composite.

Once those first references were cross-checked, they were compiled into documents to be handed to artists as actionable briefs. The airfield was ceasing to be a a vague historical subject and started to transform into a documented layout that could actually be plotted in the engine. The graybox could begin in earnest.

Grayboxing the base

With the footprint was in place, we needed to take a closer look at on-the-ground period references to providing usable metrics for traversible blockouts. Using original footage and photograpgs, I prepared reference that allowed artists to begin reconstruct classroom, office and briefing spaces and communal rooms on the base. At that stage, though, the rooms were all still hollow shells - we now had both interiors and exteriors to begin filling with assets. The design problem was now more focused on moving from establishing proportions and placement to decorating and populating believable lived spaces.

This was where grayboxing demonstated it's value - the rooms were either reading immediately or missed the mark entirely. The processes that gave us spaces had to be tested, reviewed, and iterated on before final art. Most importantly they had to be vetted against the references by myself and the other lead designer before final art.

Brass tacks - Digging in deep on esoteric material culture

With the grayboxing well underway, I went looking for the details I knew the players would really appreciate. There were tiny details that would make the base feel inhabited in 1944 rather than a simple backdrop. I started digging into personal accounts and private photographs. These proved to hold countless invaluable anecdotes and turned up the sort of references that are easy to miss but invaluable once found: improvised washroom infrastructure like reused coffee canisters hung as cigarette tins both in and outside buildings, the layout of the giant bomber scheduling chalkboard full of assignment information (absolutely critical to mission design), and the austere Air Ministry furniture and it's markings that gave offices and briefing rooms their particular period character.

This stage was probably the most fulfulling for me because the findings were exciting in more than one way. On a personal level I just love getting into the weeds on material culture, but on a practical level these were able to be critical references for 3D and environment artists trying to recreate the minutiae of daily life on the base. Additionally, they informed mission design and interface thinking. The scheduling board, in particular, was a window into how bomber operations were organized and communicated. We decided to adapt it as a kind of scoring board for the players 25 missions.

Expanding the visual story of the base

With the footprint, interiors, and material culture well established and being worked on, the next step for design was broadening the visual texture of the airfield itself. We wanted more than just thecorrect rooms. We wanted the player to feel transported into Thorpe Abbotts and its surrounding landscape. That meant pushing roughouts of interiors and exteriors further, using geographic surveys to reconstruct the countryside, roads, and wider spaces around the base. It also meant delving into the daily lives of nonmilitary personnel and learning about their schedules and jobs on the base. It was a surprise to me and many others on the team just how involved private civilians were in keeping the wheels turning on a base like Thorpe Abbotts.

That wider context mattered because it tied the intimate spaces of the base and the operational life around it together. Things were really beginning to shape up.

Physical remnants into digital reality

One of the clearest examples of payoff was a reference I found for a mobile air traffic control hut. The darn thing kept appearing in the background of reference photos, sometimes in frame but often only partly visible. We could see it often enough to know it mattered, but it was not clearly photographed well enough to understand it. It was clearly a boring everyday object to the people that knew of it because they treated it as a nonentity. This is the kind of thing that stumps anthropologists for decades. Thankfully I was able to uncover enough usable images to create a full turnaround for the vehicle artists - a real research task in itself. I was able to pinn down its role in communicating with aircraft as they taxied and landed through close review of manuals and written personal accounts. Finally, the model could move ahead with confidence.

I remember the moment vividly because it felt like my research was really paying off. There was a real buzz in the office when the model was finalized and showcased - we were all so proud. It was a small structure, but it represented the best part of our work together the project: turning important historical memories of the past which threatened to become vague and arcane into a modern experience that honours the history we were referencing.
