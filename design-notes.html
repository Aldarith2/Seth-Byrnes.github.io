Philosophy and Lessons

Not every game gets a sequel, but sequels are often the best return on investment.

Games do not always need to be complex to be fun. They only need to be complex enough for the fun they want to deliver.

Simplified systems get delivered faster with fewer iterations, and this costs less.

Consider every team member's feedback, but centralize decision-making power in the producer. No game is built by committee.

Try designing the manual before you design the game. If the instructions are easy to understand, the functionality will be easier to design clearly.

Players expect the game to do what it says on the box. Do not promise anything your game does not deliver.

To maintain good reviews on launch, games need more style than fidelity, more charm than dialogue, and fewer bugs than shipped features.

Control subjects are usually too nice in QA tests, but your boss is often succinct.

Do not wait until the end to design sound, but do wait until the last of the main functionality exists before going beyond placeholders.

The vibe is everything.

Team leads must study the demographic. If a player does not like the vibe, they will not invest money or mental facility.

Somebody at market wants your game.

Favorite Mods

The mod scene matters to me because it turns games from products into foundations. Good mods are not just extra content. They are evidence that the original work created room for authorship.

The Battle Grounds

What it gets right: One-shot lethality, committed melee, and a remarkably expressive combat rhythm.

Where it stumbles: It is rough, limited, and visually ungainly by any generous standard.

The important part is that the loop survives the roughness. It is one of the best examples I know of design outliving technology.

Insurgency

What it gets right: Lethality, tension, and disciplined tactical readability.

Where it stumbles: It can become severe enough that some of the social looseness drains out of it.

Even so, its mod-era form is still a strong reminder that focused rules and strong atmosphere can carry tremendous value.

Sven Co-op

What it gets right: It treats cooperative extension as a feature, not an afterthought, and proves how far a shared foundation can stretch.

Where it stumbles: Quality varies with the content, and that openness naturally produces uneven results.

That unevenness is part of the point. A live scene will always be messier than a sealed product, and usually more interesting.

Thief Fan Missions

What it gets right: Atmosphere, authored stealth spaces, and a decades-long culture of design literacy.

Where it stumbles: The scene has the same unevenness every long-running scene has.

At its best, it produces work that is more confident, more atmospheric, and more mechanically disciplined than many full commercial releases.
